Patient journey · Vita preventative care

From data to understanding to action.

The product's spine: three transitions, each with a deliberate design intervention. The first session moves the patient from raw data to meaning. The care plan moves the patient from meaning to action. Adherence tracking sustains the action long enough for it to show in the next test.

01
First session · Onboarding

Curated health snapshot.

3–5 most-relevant metrics from the latest test, each with a range, a trend, and a plain-language explanation. The full panel is accessible on demand — never the default. Engagement A/B testing showed meaningfully higher 30-day retention versus the full-panel default.

03
Action · Care plan

Clinical direction → patient-authored plan.

The clinical recommendation is a direction, not a mandate. The patient writes the commitment — frequency, duration, activity type — anchored to that direction. Patient-defined goals consistently outperformed clinician-mandated targets on adherence.

04
Sustaining · Adherence

Progress framing, never compliance.

"4 of 7 completed" produces continued engagement. "Missed 3 of 7" produces shame and abandonment. The component API rejects a "missed" prop — there is no compliance-framing variant in the system, by design.

05
Deepening · ML-powered insights

Recommendations with transparent confidence.

Direction-not-directive copy. Three confidence tiers as language — "Based on your results," "Consider," "Ask your doctor about." Never a raw model score. Every recommendation has Accept · Adjust · Set aside; Adjust is first-class because partial acceptance is the common case.

Principles that shaped this journey.

Established in year one of the engagement, maintained across three years and multiple product versions, and adopted as organizational defaults for how Vita communicates health data.

Principle 1

No health value without interpretation.

Every lab result, every metric, every trend needs a plain-language explanation of what it means in context. Numbers alone are either alarming or meaningless — both are product failures.

Principle 2

Progress framing, always.

Adherence tracking shows what was done, never what was missed. Same data, different framing, measurably different patient response.

Principle 3

Patient authors the plan.

Clinical recommendations are direction. The commitment is patient-defined, editable, anchored to the trend that prompted it. Patient-defined goals produce higher adherence than clinician-mandated targets.

Principle 4

Confidence as language, not a score.

Three fixed phrases — "Based on your results," "Consider," "Ask your doctor about." Patients can't act on 0.84. The tier wraps the score in language they can use to weigh the recommendation.
